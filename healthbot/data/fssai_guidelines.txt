FSSAI Guidelines for a Healthy Diet

1. Balanced Diet: A balanced diet should provide around 50-60% of total calories from carbohydrates, preferably from complex carbohydrates, about 10-15% from proteins and 20-30% from both visible and invisible fat.
2. Fruits and Vegetables: Consume at least 400g (5 portions) of fruits and vegetables per day to reduce the risk of non-communicable diseases (NCDs) and help ensure an adequate daily intake of dietary fibre.
3. Salt Intake: Keep salt intake to less than 5g per day (approx. 1 teaspoon) to prevent hypertension and reduce the risk of heart disease and stroke.
4. Sugar Intake: Free sugars should be less than 10% of total energy intake. A further reduction to less than 5% of total energy intake provides additional health benefits.
5. Fats and Oils: Limit total fat intake to less than 30% of total energy intake. Saturated fats should be less than 10% and trans fats less than 1% of total energy intake. Replace saturated fats and trans fats with unsaturated fats (found in fish, avocado and nuts, and in sunflower, soybean, canola and olive oils).
6. Hydration: Drink 8-10 glasses of water every day. Water is essential for life and is required for digestion, absorption, transportation, dissolving nutrients, elimination of waste products and thermoregulation.
7. Physical Activity: Engage in at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic physical activity throughout the week or do at least 75 minutes of vigorous-intensity aerobic physical activity throughout the week.

Specific Advice for High Blood Pressure (Hypertension):
- Reduce sodium intake significantly.
- Increase potassium intake through fruits and vegetables like bananas, oranges, and spinach.
- Maintain a healthy weight.
- Limit alcohol consumption.
- Quit smoking.

Specific Advice for Diabetes:
- Monitor carbohydrate intake.
- Choose whole grains over refined grains.
- Avoid sugary drinks and processed foods with added sugar.
- Eat meals at regular times.
- Include fiber-rich foods in every meal.

Sleep Hygiene:
- Stick to a sleep schedule of the same bedtime and wake up time, even on the weekends.
- Practice a relaxing bedtime ritual.
- Exercise daily but not close to bedtime.
- Evaluate your room. Design your sleep environment to establish the conditions you need for sleep. Cool, dark, and quiet is usually best.
- Aim for 7-9 hours of sleep per night for adults.
- Avoid caffeine and heavy meals 2-3 hours before bedtime.

Specific Advice for Heart Disease:
- Follow a heart-healthy diet rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and lean proteins.
- Limit saturated fat to less than 7% of total daily calories.
- Avoid trans fats completely.
- Reduce cholesterol intake to less than 200mg per day.
- Include omega-3 fatty acids from fish, walnuts, and flaxseeds.
- Control portion sizes to maintain healthy weight.

Specific Advice for Obesity/Weight Management:
- Create a calorie deficit by consuming fewer calories than you burn.
- Eat protein-rich foods to increase satiety and preserve muscle mass.
- Include high-fiber foods to promote fullness.
- Avoid processed foods, sugary beverages, and excessive snacking.
- Practice mindful eating and portion control.
- Combine diet with regular physical activity.
- Aim for gradual weight loss of 0.5-1 kg per week.

Specific Advice for Kidney Disease:
- Limit protein intake as advised by healthcare provider.
- Restrict sodium to less than 2g per day.
- Monitor potassium and phosphorus intake.
- Limit fluid intake if recommended by doctor.
- Avoid processed foods high in sodium and phosphorus additives.
- Choose fresh fruits and vegetables with lower potassium content.

Food Safety and Hygiene:
- Wash hands thoroughly before handling food.
- Keep raw and cooked foods separate to avoid cross-contamination.
- Cook food to safe internal temperatures (poultry: 75°C, ground meat: 70°C).
- Refrigerate perishable foods within 2 hours.
- Use safe water and raw materials.
- Avoid consuming expired or spoiled food products.

Food Adulteration Detection Methods:

Milk:
- Water: Put a drop on a slanted surface; pure milk flows slowly
- Starch: Add iodine; blue color indicates starch
- Urea: Add soybean powder; ammonia smell indicates urea

Ghee/Butter:
- Starch: Heat and add iodine; blue color indicates starch
- Vanaspati: Rub on palm; pure ghee melts completely

Honey:
- Water: Pure honey doesn't dissolve quickly in water
- Sugar syrup: Add methylated spirit; milky appearance indicates adulteration

Turmeric Powder:
- Add water; pure turmeric settles, adulterants float
- Add hydrochloric acid; pink color indicates metanil yellow

Chili Powder:
- Add water; artificial color will separate and float
- Brick powder: Sprinkle on water surface; brick powder sinks

Tea/Coffee:
- Add cold water; artificial color releases immediately

Oil:
- Refrigerate; pure oil remains clear, adulterated oil becomes cloudy

Sugar:
- Dissolve in water; chalk powder will settle at bottom

Glycemic Index (GI) Food Classification:

Low GI Foods (GI < 55):
- Most fruits (apple, pear, orange, berries)
- Non-starchy vegetables
- Legumes and pulses
- Whole grains (oats, barley, quinoa)
- Nuts and seeds
- Benefits: Better blood sugar control, sustained energy

Medium GI Foods (GI 56-69):
- Whole wheat products
- Brown rice
- Sweet potato
- Banana (ripe)
- Raisins

High GI Foods (GI > 70):
- White bread, white rice
- Potatoes (baked, mashed)
- Cornflakes, puffed rice
- Watermelon
- Glucose, sugar
- Limit these for better blood sugar management

Food Myths and Facts:

Myth: Carbs are bad and cause weight gain
Fact: Complex carbs are essential; excess calories cause weight gain

Myth: Eating fat makes you fat
Fact: Healthy fats are essential; excess calories from any source cause weight gain

Myth: Detox diets cleanse your body
Fact: Liver and kidneys naturally detoxify; eat balanced diet

Myth: Organic food is more nutritious
Fact: Similar nutrition; organic has less pesticide residue

Myth: Eating late at night causes weight gain
Fact: Total daily calories matter more than timing

Myth: Supplements can replace healthy diet
Fact: Whole foods provide nutrients in best form

Understanding Blood Sugar Levels:
- Normal fasting: 70-100 mg/dL
- Prediabetes fasting: 100-125 mg/dL
- Diabetes fasting: ≥126 mg/dL
- Normal post-meal (2 hours): <140 mg/dL
- Prediabetes post-meal: 140-199 mg/dL
- Diabetes post-meal: ≥200 mg/dL

Low Glycemic Index Foods:
- Legumes: Lentils, chickpeas, kidney beans
- Non-starchy vegetables: Broccoli, cauliflower, leafy greens
- Whole grains: Oats, barley, quinoa
- Nuts and seeds
- Most fruits: Berries, apples, pears, oranges
- Dairy: Milk, yogurt

Blood Sugar Balancing Strategies:
- Eat protein with every meal
- Include healthy fats for satiety
- Never eat carbs alone
- Eat vegetables first, then protein, then carbs
- Stay physically active after meals
- Manage stress levels
- Get adequate sleep (7-9 hours)

Foods That Spike Blood Sugar:
- White bread, white rice
- Sugary drinks and sodas
- Pastries and cakes
- Candy and sweets
- Processed breakfast cereals
- Fruit juices (even 100%)
- Potatoes (especially mashed or baked)

Carbohydrate Understanding:

Types of Carbohydrates:
Simple Carbohydrates:
- Sugars: Glucose, fructose, sucrose
- Quick energy but rapid blood sugar spike
- Found in: Fruits, milk, table sugar, honey

Complex Carbohydrates:
- Starches: Long chains of glucose
- Slower digestion, sustained energy
- Found in: Grains, legumes, vegetables
- Preferred carbohydrate source

Fiber (Non-digestible carb):
- Soluble: Dissolves in water, lowers cholesterol
- Insoluble: Adds bulk, aids digestion
- Found in: Whole grains, fruits, vegetables, legumes

Carbohydrate Quality:
High Quality:
- Whole grains: Brown rice, quinoa, oats
- Legumes: Beans, lentils, peas
- Vegetables: All types
- Fruits: Whole fruits with skin
- Benefits: Fiber, vitamins, minerals, phytochemicals

Low Quality:
- Refined grains: White bread, white rice
- Sugary drinks and sweets
- Processed snacks
- Pastries and desserts
- Minimal nutrients, rapid blood sugar spike

Carb Timing:
- Morning: Moderate to high (energy for day)

- Post-workout: Moderate to high (recovery)
- Evening: Lower (reduced activity)
- Adjust based on activity level

Fat Facts and Functions:

Types of Fats:
Saturated Fats:
- Solid at room temperature
- Sources: Butter, ghee, coconut oil, fatty meats
- Limit to <10% of calories
- Raises LDL cholesterol

Unsaturated Fats (Healthy):
Monounsaturated:
- Liquid at room temperature
- Sources: Olive oil, avocados, nuts
- Lowers LDL, maintains HDL
- Heart-healthy

Polyunsaturated:
- Omega-3: Fish, flaxseeds, walnuts (anti-inflammatory)
- Omega-6: Vegetable oils, nuts (pro-inflammatory in excess)
- Balance omega-3 to omega-6 ratio
- Essential fatty acids

Trans Fats (Avoid):
- Partially hydrogenated oils
- Found in: Fried foods, baked goods, margarine
- Raises LDL, lowers HDL
- Increases heart disease risk
- Avoid completely

Fat Functions:
- Energy storage (9 calories per gram)
- Hormone production
- Cell membrane structure

- Cellular function

Hydration Needs:
- Men: 3.7 liters/day (total fluids)

Signs of Dehydration:

- Thirst

- Very dark urine or no urination

Food Sensitivities and Elimination Diet:

Food Sensitivity vs Allergy:
Food Allergy:
- Immune system reaction (IgE)
- Immediate symptoms (minutes to hours)
- Can be life-threatening (anaphylaxis)
- Common: Peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, eggs, milk
- Requires strict avoidance

Food Sensitivity/Intolerance:
- Non-immune reaction or IgG response
- Delayed symptoms (hours to days)
- Not life-threatening but uncomfortable
- Common: Lactose, gluten, FODMAPs, histamine
- May tolerate small amounts

Common Symptoms:
- Digestive: Bloating, gas, diarrhea, constipation
- Skin: Rashes, eczema, acne
- Respiratory: Congestion, runny nose
- Neurological: Headaches, brain fog, fatigue
- Joint pain and inflammation

Elimination Diet Protocol:
Phase 1 - Elimination (2-4 weeks):
- Remove common triggers: Gluten, dairy, eggs, soy, corn, nuts, shellfish
- Keep detailed food diary
- Note symptom changes
- Eat whole, unprocessed foods

Phase 2 - Reintroduction (1 food every 3-4 days):
- Introduce one food at a time
- Eat normal portion 2-3 times in one day
- Monitor symptoms for 3 days
- If no reaction, food is likely safe
- If reaction occurs, eliminate and wait for symptoms to resolve

Phase 3 - Maintenance:
- Create personalized diet avoiding triggers
- May retry eliminated foods after 6 months
- Some sensitivities resolve over time
- Work with healthcare provider

- Trillions of bacteria in digestive tract
- Affects: Digestion, immunity, mood, weight, chronic disease
- Diversity is key to health
- Influenced by: Diet, antibiotics, stress, sleep

Prebiotics (Feed Good Bacteria):
- Fiber-rich foods
- Bananas, onions, garlic, leeks
- Asparagus, artichokes
- Whole grains, legumes
- Apples, berries

Probiotics (Good Bacteria):
- Yogurt with live cultures
- Kefir, buttermilk
- Fermented foods: Idli, dosa, dhokla
- Pickles (naturally fermented)
- Kimchi, sauerkraut
- Kombucha

Gut-Damaging Factors:
- Antibiotics: Kill good and bad bacteria
- Processed foods: Low fiber, high sugar
- Artificial sweeteners: May alter microbiome
- Chronic stress: Affects gut bacteria
- Lack of sleep: Disrupts gut health
- Excessive alcohol: Damages gut lining

Improving Gut Health:
- Eat diverse plant foods (30+ types/week)
- Include fermented foods daily
- Adequate fiber (25-30g/day)
- Limit processed foods and sugar
- Manage stress (meditation, yoga)
- Adequate sleep (7-9 hours)
- Avoid unnecessary antibiotics
- Stay hydrated

- Increased intestinal permeability
- Allows toxins into bloodstream
- May contribute to: Autoimmune conditions, allergies, inflammation
- Support with: Bone broth, glutamine, probiotics, anti-inflammatory diet
- Avoid: Alcohol, NSAIDs, processed foods
